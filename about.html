About This Project

Whale Watchers Atlas combines 20 years of scientific observation data from 11 whale species with curated whale watching location information. Explore animated density heatmaps showing species diversity hotspots, track individual species migration patterns, and discover the best places and times to observe these magnificent creatures.

Whale Observation Data

The heatmaps and species distribution visualizations are powered by occurrence data from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF), one of the world's largest open-access biodiversity databases.

GBIF - Global Biodiversity Information Facility

gbif.org ‚Äî Open-access database containing millions of species occurrence records contributed by researchers, citizen scientists, and institutions worldwide.

Data Processing Pipeline

Raw GBIF occurrence records are downloaded as individual species datasets, filtered for quality (coordinate precision, date accuracy), and processed using Python and GeoPandas. The H3 hexagonal grid system spatially aggregates observations, calculates species density, and applies persistence filtering before exporting to GeoJSON format for web visualization.

The density map combines observation data from 11 whale species spanning 20 years (2005-2025):

Whale Watching Location Data

The 38 whale watching destinations were curated from multiple sources including tourism boards, whale watching operator associations, scientific literature on cetacean distribution, and verified traveler reports. Location data includes:

  • Geographic coordinates and regional classification
  • Species present and their seasonal availability
  • Industry size (number of operators and vessels)
  • Annual visitor estimates from tourism statistics
  • Accessibility ratings based on transportation infrastructure

Experience Score Components

The Experience Score (labeled "Entertainment" in the data) is a composite metric considering:

  • Behavior Diversity (25%) ‚Äî Variety of observable behaviors (breaching, feeding, singing, social interaction)
  • Proximity Quality (25%) ‚Äî Typical distance to whales; some locations allow closer approaches
  • Species Diversity (20%) ‚Äî Number of different whale species regularly observed
  • Unique Features (15%) ‚Äî Special phenomena like bubble-net feeding, friendly whale interactions, or beach hunting
  • Supporting Wildlife (15%) ‚Äî Presence of other marine life (dolphins, seals, seabirds) enhancing the experience

Sighting Rate Components

The Sighting Rate (labeled "Visibility" in the data) factors in:

  • Success Rate (40%) ‚Äî Reported whale sighting success from operators (many guarantee 90%+ during peak season)
  • Season Length (20%) ‚Äî Longer reliable seasons indicate more stable populations
  • Weather Reliability (20%) ‚Äî Frequency of weather-related trip cancellations
  • Water Clarity (10%) ‚Äî Visibility conditions affecting underwater viewing where applicable
  • Whale Density (10%) ‚Äî Typical number of whales encountered per trip

Score Interpretation Guide

90-100: World-class destination, exceptional experiences
80-89: Excellent destination, highly recommended
70-79: Very good destination, reliable experiences
60-69: Good destination, solid choice for whale watching
Below 60: Emerging or seasonal destination with variable results

  • 1: Low activity ‚Äî Some whales present, unpredictable sightings

Best Months Designation

The "Best Months" for each location are determined by combining peak whale density periods with favorable weather conditions and operator availability. These represent the optimal planning window for visitors.

üî¨ Density Heatmap Creation Process

The combined species density heatmap uses an advanced hexagonal grid system to eliminate visual artifacts and provide accurate representation of whale species diversity across the globe.

Hexagonal Grid System (H3)

Instead of traditional square grid tiles that can create "striped" artifacts in heatmaps, we use Uber's H3 hexagonal hierarchical spatial index. The density map uses resolution 4 hexagons with approximately 47km edge length, providing optimal balance between regional detail and computational efficiency.

Why Hexagons?

Hexagonal grids have uniform neighbor distances and no orientation bias, making them superior to square grids for spatial aggregation. Each hexagon has exactly 6 neighbors at equal distances, preventing the directional artifacts common in square grid systems.

Processing Pipeline

The density heatmap generation involves several sophisticated steps:

  1. Data Collection: GBIF observation data from 11 whale species across 20 years (2005-2025)
  2. Temporal Binning: Observations assigned to 12 monthly bins based on event dates
  3. Spatial Assignment: Each observation mapped to an H3 hexagonal cell using lat/lon coordinates
  4. Species Density Calculation: Count unique species per hexagon per month (density: 1-11)
  5. Persistence Filtering: Remove isolated single-month appearances to eliminate noise
  6. Point Generation: Generate 10 random points per hexagon with density property
  7. Ocean Clipping: Remove land-based points using Natural Earth ocean boundaries
  8. GeoJSON Export: Final dataset with density property for Mapbox heatmap-weight visualization

Persistence Threshold Filter

To reduce noise from single opportunistic sightings, the pipeline applies a temporal persistence filter. Cell-month combinations are removed if they are completely isolated (no observations in the previous or next month at that location). This ensures the heatmap shows consistent seasonal patterns rather than random single occurrences.

Species Density vs. Observation Count

Key Distinction

The heatmap shows species diversity (how many different species overlap), not raw observation counts. A cell with 3 species counts the same whether there are 5 or 500 individual observations. Brighter areas indicate where more species co-occur, revealing biodiversity hotspots and migration corridors.

üó∫Ô∏è Map Technology

The interactive maps are built using Mapbox GL JS, a powerful JavaScript library for rendering vector maps with WebGL. This enables smooth animations, dynamic styling, and efficient handling of large datasets.

Heatmap Visualization

The density heatmaps use Mapbox's heatmap layer with the density property as the heatmap-weight. This creates smooth gradients showing species diversity patterns. The monthly animation smoothly transitions between months to visualize migration patterns throughout the year.

Important Note on Heatmaps

Heatmap density reflects observation effort as much as actual whale presence. Areas with more whale watching activity, research stations, or shipping traffic will have more recorded sightings. Remote areas may have fewer records despite healthy whale populations.

‚ö†Ô∏è Data Limitations & Caveats

  • Observation Bias: GBIF data is contributed by researchers, citizen scientists, and institutions. Coverage is uneven globally, with better data in well-studied regions.
  • Temporal Gaps: Historical data quality varies. Recent years (2015-present) generally have better coverage than earlier periods.
  • Species Identification: Some records may have identification uncertainties, particularly for species that are difficult to distinguish at sea.
  • Location Ratings: Scores are based on available information and may not reflect recent changes in whale populations or tourism infrastructure.
  • Seasonal Variation: Whale presence can vary significantly year-to-year based on oceanographic conditions, prey availability, and climate patterns.
